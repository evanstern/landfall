01

The 20-Second Bet

What landfall does, and the one-line formula at its heart

What is this project?

landfall is not an app you open — it is a library, written in Go, with exactly one job:

🧭
The one job

Before your app spends money and time on a slow, expensive question — to an LLM, a heavy planner, even a human approver — landfall decides: will the answer still be true by the time it arrives? If not, do not ask.

⚡
A fast loop advances the world

Your app keeps moving: a user keeps typing, a market keeps trading, a robot keeps rolling.

🔮
A slow oracle answers with delay

An oracle takes seconds — or hours — to reply. The world does not wait for it.

🚪
landfall is the gate between them

It prices the bet before the call is spent, and re-checks it when the answer lands.

The bet you didn’t know you were making

Imagine you’re driving and you phone a friend who is very smart but very slow: “Which exit do I take?” They think for thirty seconds. By the time they answer — you’ve already passed the exit.

1

You ask while the car is here

→
2

The car keeps moving while they think

→
3

The answer arrives about a road you’ve already left

Every slow question is secretly this bet: the world when the answer lands will still resemble the world when I asked. Sometimes that bet is safe — on a long empty highway, a thirty-second answer is fine. In dense city traffic, it is worthless before it arrives.

💡
Aha! Refuse the bet before you pay, not after

Most software copes with slowness after paying for it — timeouts and fallbacks. landfall refuses bets it can predict will lose, so the money and the waiting are never spent at all. Engineers call this being ex-ante (before the fact) instead of ex-post (after it).

The whole library in one line of arithmetic

landfall prices the bet with grade-school math. No AI inside, no guessing — just multiplication and one comparison:

THE FORMULA 

predicted drift = velocity × time-to-land
allow iff predicted drift ≤ budget
PLAIN ENGLISH

How much will the world change while we wait? Speed of change, times how long the answer takes.

Ask only if that predicted change fits inside what this decision can survive.

🌊

Velocity

How fast the world is changing right now, measured in salient events per second. Highway cruising is low velocity; city traffic is high.

⏱️

Time-to-land

Predicted seconds until the answer arrives. Named for the moment an answer “lands” back in your app — hence landfall.

📐

Predicted drift

The product of the two: how many salient events will pile up while the answer is in flight.

🛡️

Budget

How much change this kind of decision survives. Budget 5 reads: “this answer stays useful through 5 salient events.” It’s the answer’s half-life.

The real code — exactly as it ships

Here is the heart of the gate, from route.go. This is the actual shipping code, not a cleaned-up version:

route.go 

	v.PredictedWallSec = float64(c.Points) * secondsPerPoint
	v.EffectiveBudget = c.Budget * (1 + debt*c.DebtFactor)
	if velocity < 0 || math.IsNaN(velocity) || math.IsInf(velocity, 0) {
		v.Arithmetic = fmt.Sprintf("%dpt x %.1fs/pt at unmeasurable velocity (%v) - suppressed", c.Points, secondsPerPoint, velocity)
		return v
	}
	v.PredictedDrift = v.PredictedWallSec * velocity
	v.Allow = v.PredictedDrift <= v.EffectiveBudget
PLAIN ENGLISH

Predict how long the call will take: the size of the ask times measured seconds per unit of size.

Compute the budget, with a fairness boost for decisions that keep getting told no — we’ll meet this “debt” in Module 5.

If the speed of the world is unmeasurable — negative, or a broken number — do not guess…

…write down exactly why we refused, in human-readable math…

…and answer “no” right here. When in doubt, refuse.

Close the safety check.

The formula itself: predicted change = predicted wait × world speed.

Allow the call only if predicted change fits in the budget. That single comparison is the whole gate.

⏸️
Zero means paused, and paused is always safe

If velocity is 0, the world is standing still — anything × 0 = 0 drift, so the call is always allowed. But an unmeasurable velocity is refused outright. The doctrine: when the system doesn’t know, it fails toward caution, never toward acting on stale information.

Meet the Cast

Five characters run this courtroom — and your app is one of them

A courtroom, not a casino

landfall works like a very disciplined courtroom. There’s a law book, a judge, and a written ruling for every single case — including, especially, the refusals. Nothing is decided on vibes.

📕
Class — the law book entry

Each kind of decision is registered up front: its name, its size, its budget, and its declared fallback. Defined in class.go.

⚖️
Route — the judge

A pure function in route.go that applies the law book to the evidence and rules allow or refuse.

📜
Verdict — the written ruling

Not just yes/no: every number that went into the decision, plus the equation itself as a human-readable sentence.

⏱️
Estimator — the timing expert

The one witness who learns: a live estimate of how slow the oracle currently is (estimate.go). Module 4 is all about it.

🏛️
The host — your app, the court clerk

landfall is a library, remember — your app measures the world, brings the evidence, files every ruling, and carries out the sentence.

The law book: registering a Class

Here is the actual struct from class.go. One of these is written, reviewed, and registered for every kind of oracle-reaching decision your app makes:

class.go 

type Class struct {
	Name    string
	Points  int
	Budget  float64
	Degrade Degrade
	// DebtFactor governs starvation relief: each suppressed call accrues one
	// unit of debt, and the effective budget is Budget × (1 + debt×DebtFactor),
	// so persistent suppression eventually admits a call. Zero means starvation
	// is acceptable for this class (e.g. purely cosmetic decisions).
	DebtFactor float64
}
PLAIN ENGLISH

A Class bundles everything the judge needs to know about one kind of decision.

Its name — like “planner” or “autocomplete” — so every ruling says who it was about.

Its size in points: how big an ask this is, independent of which oracle answers it. A one-liner might be 1 point; a deep analysis, 8.

Its budget: how many salient events the answer survives. A reviewed constant, never auto-tuned.

Its declared fallback for when the gate says no — more on this below.

The comments are part of the real file: they explain the fairness mechanism…

…each refusal accrues “debt” that inflates the budget…

…so a decision that keeps losing eventually wins one…

…and zero opts out, for decisions where starving is fine.

The knob itself — Module 5 puts numbers on it.

End of the law book entry.

That Degrade field is landfall’s quiet masterstroke. A Class cannot be registered without naming a legal fallback — validation fails. Four are allowed:

🤫

skip

Do nothing — but record that nothing was done. Never silent.

🦎

reflex

A simple, instant, rule-based policy covers — the lizard brain that never waits.

📋

template

A pre-written answer stands in — authored ahead of time, always available.

🏃

faster-tier

Retry against a quicker (smaller) oracle whose answer will land sooner.

💡
Aha! Graceful degradation by construction

Most systems bolt fallbacks on after an outage embarrasses them. Here, installing the gate forces the fallback to exist — you literally cannot register a slow-oracle decision without declaring what happens when it’s refused. Ask your AI tools for this: “make the fallback a required field, not an afterthought.”

What landfall refuses to do

landfall is deliberately a leaf — it has zero dependencies and keeps its job tiny. Everything below is your app’s responsibility, on purpose:

velocity Measuring how fast the world changes — you estimate it outside and pass a number in
recording Persisting every Verdict and outcome — landfall writes rulings, you keep the archive
debt Counting refusals and resetting the counter after a success
failures Retries and outage handling belong to a circuit breaker, not the gate
🧩
Separation of concerns

Splitting “decide” from “measure, record, and recover” is a universal engineering move called separation of concerns. It keeps each piece small enough to test perfectly. When an AI assistant offers to fold everything into one clever component — that’s the moment to push back.

03

The Flight

Takeoff clearance is not landing clearance — how an allowed call is checked again when it returns

Cleared for takeoff ≠ cleared to land

An airplane gets two separate clearances: one to take off, and a fresh one to land — because the weather at the destination may have changed during the flight. landfall works exactly the same way. The Gate’s “allow” was a prediction; while the oracle thinks, the world keeps moving.

1
Checkout — takeoff

The moment an allowed call launches, a Lease freezes a snapshot: which class flew, the budget it was allowed against, the drift the Gate bet on, and the world’s generation number.

2
Flight — the world moves on

The oracle thinks. Meanwhile drift accumulates, and if something truly major happens, the host bumps the generation counter.

3
Land — the second clearance

When the answer arrives, Land compares the frozen snapshot against reality and rules: apply it, or don’t — with the numbers to prove it.

The flight recorder: Checkout

The entire takeoff procedure is three lines in lease.go:

lease.go 

func Checkout(v Verdict, gen uint64) Lease {
	return Lease{Gen: gen, Class: v.Class, Budget: v.EffectiveBudget, PredictedDrift: v.PredictedDrift}
}
PLAIN ENGLISH

At launch, take the allowed Verdict and the current generation number…

…and freeze four facts into the Lease: the generation at takeoff, who flew, the budget it was judged against, and the drift the Gate predicted.

That’s it — a tiny, tamper-proof boarding pass.

🧊
Why freeze the prediction inside the lease?

Because carrying the bet with the flight means every landing can compare “what the Gate predicted” against “what actually happened” in one record — no digging through old logs to match them up. Databases call this style optimistic concurrency control: proceed hopefully, validate at the end.

Three ways to land

✅

Landed

Same generation, drift within budget. The world still matches — apply the answer.

🔄

Superseded

A salient event bumped the generation mid-flight. Don’t apply it — but you may quietly warm a cache with it.

⏰

Stale

No supersession, but actual drift blew past the budget — the Gate’s prediction was wrong. Record it loudly.

And the landing check itself, verbatim from lease.go:

lease.go 

func (l Lease) Land(currentGen uint64, actualDrift float64) Landing {
	ld := Landing{
		Class: l.Class, Gen: l.Gen, CurrentGen: currentGen,
		Budget: l.Budget, PredictedDrift: l.PredictedDrift, ActualDrift: actualDrift,
	}
	switch {
	case currentGen != l.Gen:
		ld.Outcome = Superseded
	case actualDrift > l.Budget:
		ld.Outcome = Stale
	default:
		ld.Outcome = Landed
	}
	return ld
}
PLAIN ENGLISH

The landing clearance: hand in the world as it is now — current generation and the drift that actually accumulated.

Start writing the Landing record…

…copying in everything frozen at takeoff…

…plus the prediction and what really happened — side by side in one record.

Snapshot complete.

Now the ruling — checked in strict priority order:

Did the generation change during the flight?

Then something big happened: Superseded — no matter how small the drift was.

Otherwise, did real drift exceed the budget?

Then the Gate’s bet lost: Stale.

Neither?

Safe to apply: Landed.

End of the ruling.

Return the record — every landing gets one, even the failures. Especially the failures.

Done: pure arithmetic, same inputs, same outcome, every time.

💡
Aha! Supersession trumps staleness

Notice the switch order: the generation check comes first. If the big event happened, the answer is dead regardless of how little drift accrued — and every dropped answer leaves a record. A silently dropped answer is how “why is the assistant so passive?” mystery bugs are born.

The report card that writes itself

Because every Landing carries PredictedDrift and ActualDrift in the same units, the landing log doubles as the Gate’s report card — for free.

predicted 4.5 / actual 4.1 Healthy: the Gate’s bets are landing close to reality
predicted 4.5 / actual 9.8 One bad bet — noise happens, keep watching
predicted < actual, every day A pattern of Stale outcomes: the velocity measurement or the timing calibration is lying — a human should fix the inputs

A call checks out at generation 12 with budget 8. It lands at generation 12 with actual drift 3. What’s the outcome?

Checkout at generation 7; while the oracle thinks, one salient event bumps it to 8. The answer lands with a tiny actual drift of 0.5, well under budget. Outcome?

The Estimator That Won’t Be Fooled

The one learning part of landfall — and the strict rules that keep it honest

Where does “time-to-land” come from?

The Gate multiplies velocity by predicted call time — but who predicts the call time? Think of how you predict your commute: the trip has a distance (fixed) and a pace (changes with traffic). landfall splits latency the same way:

📏

Points — the distance. How big the ask is. A property of the question, fixed at registration.

×
🚦

Seconds-per-point — the pace. How slow this oracle currently is. Measured live by the Estimator.

=
⏱️

Time-to-land — the prediction the Gate multiplies by velocity.

💡
Aha! Factoring means new decisions start smart

Because size and speed are separate, a brand-new decision class needs zero timing history — assign it points and it inherits the oracle’s measured pace, the way you can estimate a brand-new errand across town because you already know the traffic. One estimator per oracle, not one per decision.

A memory that bends but doesn’t break

You know your commute is about 30 minutes. One day a crash makes it 90 — you don’t start planning 90 every day. But when new roadwork adds 5 minutes every day, your gut slowly updates. That exact instinct is six lines of estimate.go:

estimate.go 

	spike := secPerPoint > e.cfg.SpikeFactor*e.estimate
	if spike {
		e.spikes++
	} else {
		e.estimate = (1-e.cfg.Alpha)*e.estimate + e.cfg.Alpha*secPerPoint
	}
PLAIN ENGLISH

Is this new timing more than 3× what we believe? Then it’s a spike — the freak traffic jam.

If it’s a spike…

…count it (we never forget it happened) but do NOT let it move the estimate.

Otherwise, it’s a normal day…

…so blend it in gently: keep 80% of the old belief, adopt 20% of the new reading.

Freak events can’t poison the estimate; steady change still steers it.

That 80/20 blend is called an EWMA. Old evidence never vanishes; it just fades.

The rules of evidence

Two strict doctrines keep this witness honest — both are one-sentence rules you can demand in any system you build:

✅

Learn from successes only

A failed call’s duration says nothing about how long a good answer takes. Failures go to the circuit breaker, never the timing model.

🐢

When ignorant, assume slow

A fresh estimator is seeded pessimistically — assumed slower than reality. An uncalibrated system refuses calls and falls back, rather than acting on stale answers.

And the tuned numbers ship right in the file — carried over from the project this library was distilled from:

estimate.go 

func DefaultEstimatorConfig() EstimatorConfig {
	return EstimatorConfig{Alpha: 0.2, SpikeFactor: 3.0, WindowSize: 20, BreachRate: 0.3}
}
PLAIN ENGLISH

The battle-tested default settings, as one ready-made bundle:

Blend 20% of each new reading; call anything over 3× the estimate a spike; watch the last 20 samples; and worry when more than 30% of them are spikes.

Done — hosts start here and only change these numbers deliberately.

The smoke alarm

One freak jam is noise. But if 6 of your last 20 commutes were “freak” jams… they’re not freak anymore — your whole mental map is stale. The Estimator watches its recent window, and when the spike rate crosses 30% it raises a one-time signal: recalibrate me.

1
Spikes are excluded, but counted

Every sample marks a slot in a rolling 20-slot window: spike or normal.

2
The window rate is the tell

Spike rate above 30% over a full window means the baseline itself is lying — the signal fires once, and re-arms only after things recover.

3
A human moves the baseline

The recorded calibration is only ever changed by a person. The live estimator adapts; the official record never drifts silently.

🧑‍⚖️
Machines adapt, humans commit

This split — a live estimate that adapts freely, a recorded baseline only a human may change — shows up in great production systems everywhere. It’s a pattern worth asking your AI tools for by name: “let it adapt at runtime, but never let it rewrite its own configured baseline.”

Debt, Tiers & the Empty Gate

How nobody starves, how the best affordable oracle wins — and why the gate contains no AI at all

The starvation problem

Picture a store that rain-checks you every time an item is sold out — and each refusal makes your voucher bigger. Refuse you often enough and eventually your voucher is so generous that even on a packed day, you get served. That’s suppression debt.

Why it’s needed: if the world stays hot for hours, the Gate keeps saying no, the oracle is never consulted, and your app flatlines at fallback quality forever. Debt guarantees an eventual yes:

debt 0 → budget 5.0 Predicted drift is 9 — refused. The class accrues one unit of debt.
debt 1 → budget 7.5 Budget 5 × (1 + 1 × 0.5) = 7.5. Still under 9 — refused again, debt grows.
debt 2 → budget 10.0 5 × (1 + 2 × 0.5) = 10 — drift 9 now fits. Admitted. A successful landing resets the debt to zero.
📈
The gate stays pure

Notice landfall doesn’t remember the debt — your app counts refusals and passes the number in, and the Verdict records it. Same inputs, same ruling, always. (This is the “fairness boost” you glimpsed in Module 1’s code.)

A ladder of oracles

Often you have several oracles for the same job: a brilliant slow cloud model, a decent quick local one. RouteTiered runs the gate against each and picks the best-quality tier whose answer would land in time:

🐋
Calm world → the big slow oracle

Plenty of budget headroom — take the smartest answer available.

🐇
Hot world → the small fast oracle

The deep answer would land stale; the quick one still lands true.

🦎
Nothing fits → the declared fallback

The reflex, template, or skip from Module 2 — always available, already declared.

route.go 

	chosen, ok := best, false
	for i, t := range tiers {
		v := Route(c, velocity, t.SecondsPerPoint, t.Debt)
		if !v.Allow {
			continue
		}
		if !ok || t.Quality > tiers[chosen].Quality {
			chosen, ok = i, true
		}
	}
PLAIN ENGLISH

Start with no winner chosen.

Try every rung of the ladder, one by one…

…running the exact same pure gate from Module 1 on each tier — each with its own pace and its own debt.

If this tier’s answer would land stale…

…skip it and move on.

(No exceptions — a tier that can’t land in time is out.)

Among the tiers that do fit, prefer the highest quality…

…and remember it as the current winner.

Keep the best fit as we go.

Done: smartest oracle that still lands true — or nothing, and the fallback runs.

💡
Aha! Each tier keeps its own tab

Debt is per-tier: a quick local answer does not repay the debt owed to the suppressed cloud tier. In a persistently hot world, the cloud tier’s voucher keeps growing until a cloud call finally fits — so the system escapes not just “never consulted,” but “forever stuck on the cheap answer.”

The empty gate

Here’s the design decision that makes everything else trustworthy. The judge that governs the AI contains, deliberately, nothing:

🕰️

No clock

It never reads the time — every duration is handed in as a number.

🎲

No randomness

Same inputs, same verdict. Every time. Forever.

🚫

No model

No AI inside the gate — so the gate can’t hallucinate itself open, and no clever prompt injection can talk it open either.

🧾

No hidden state

Every ruling is reproducible from its own recorded fields — a property-based test re-derives each verdict from its own record to prove it.

🏛️
The gate that governs the model contains no model

This is the sentence to steal. When AI decides what to answer, something deterministic should decide whether to ask. Tell your AI tools: “keep the enforcement path pure — no model, no clock, no randomness in the code that says yes or no.”

Same reasoning behind one last refusal: budgets have no runtime tuning API. They are doctrine — constants a human reviews — because a system that quietly re-tunes its own limits is a model sneaking back into the enforcement path through the side door. The landing log (Module 3’s report card) tells humans when a constant is wrong.

Where this pattern travels

Nothing in the formula mentions AI. Any “fast world, slow expensive answer” problem fits:

📝

AI on live documents

Velocity = edits per second. Is the 20-second deep analysis worth it, or will the user type right through it?

📊

Trading

Velocity = market volatility; budget = how long an edge stays profitable. Identical formula.

🤖

Robotics

Heavy route planner vs. reactive controller — budget measured in how far the plan can diverge.

🧑‍💼

Human approvers

An approver is an oracle with hours of latency. Approval queues everywhere are full of stale asks nobody priced.

And that’s the whole machine: a pure gate that prices the bet, a lease that re-checks it at landing, an estimator that learns carefully, and debt that keeps anyone from starving — every decision leaving a receipt you can replay.
